Ensemble Organum Full Overview and Wiki

Formation and Musical Heritage

Ensemble Organum, a prestigious name in the world of early music, was co-founded in 1982 by Marcel Pérès in France.

The group has been a pioneer in reviving medieval music, focusing on a repertoire that spans from Gregorian chants to Mozarabic and Old Roman chants.

This choice set them apart from contemporary classical music ensembles and sparked interest among classical and early music enthusiasts.

Musical Innovations and Influence

The ensemble’s music is profoundly influenced by Orthodox music traditions, with Marcel Pérès’s leadership steering the group through explorations of lesser-known liturgical music.

They’ve worked closely with figures like Lycourgos Angelopoulos, enhancing their authenticity and depth.

They have been instrumental in popularizing chants associated with various European medieval traditions, including Carolingian and Gallican chants.

Collaborative Efforts and Locations

Originally based at Sénanque Abbey and later at Royaumont Abbey, the group moved in 2001 to Moissac Abbey, sharing facilities with the Centre itinérant de recherche sur les musiques anciennes.

This relocation marked a significant phase in their journey, combining performance with academic research, which has been a cornerstone of their enduring legacy.

Discography and Contributions to Music

With a discography primarily released by Harmonia Mundi, their recordings have been critical in understanding medieval and early music’s evolution.

FAQs

What is Ensemble Organum?

This is a French musical group specializing in early medieval music, founded in 1982 by Marcel Pérès. They focus on reviving ancient liturgical chant and polyphony from the Middle Ages.

Who founded Ensemble Organum?

Marcel Pérès co-founded this group in 1982 at the Abbey of Senanque, and the group is now based at the Foundation Royaumont near Paris.

What type of music do they perform?

They perform a wide range of medieval sacred music, including Gregorian, Old Roman, Byzantine, Ambrosian, Mozarabic, and Gallican chants.

Have they received any notable recognitions?

Yes, their recordings, particularly the Messe de Nostre Dame by Guillaume de Machaut, have been highly praised and included in influential lists like the New York Times’ 100 recordings that revolutionized 20th-century music.
